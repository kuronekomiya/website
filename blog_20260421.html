Writing and
'the point' of it all;
Thinking of birds

Tuesday, 21st of April, 2026

Writing is such a joy! I’ve been learning a lot lately — probably more so since I started writing back in November. It’s good to have a record of my progress. It shifts the attention from ‘knowing’ to ‘thinking’, in other words, it cherishes the process and the questions which arise. There is no end-goal, there is only this present sentence.

I’ve been thinking about something, a mundane thing, which happened in a birding trip a week ago. At the end of it, we all gathered to conclude the day and our instructor said he likes to highlight a “bird of the day” based on everyone’s thoughts. As we were all saying which bird had been our favourite someone asked “what is this for? the point.” He was genuine and wondered where this activity was going. “The point?” thought I, “what do you mean ‘the point’? Isn’t fun the reason why we are here? The point is this moment. Not working toward a future nor dissecting the past. It is for this moment and nothing else. We don’t have to take anything away, no new knowledge or even memories.” The bulk of our days are generally pointless, so much of what we do is simply for the sake of the present moment. So much of what enriches my life doesn’t carry over into the future. The point of petting a cat on my way somewhere is that I am petting a cat. There is no greater purpose. The point of writing is to write, as a stand alone activity. In a way, the point is that these things are temporary; the bird of the day will be decided, the cat will walk away, I will put my pen down as life so often interrupts itself — leaving our attention grasping for the next bit of meaning in a largely meaningless world.

Now, with this I am not saying the point of life is its finale, but that it is through knowing that —realising our own mortality— that we can proclaim “the point of this must be the space I occupy at this present moment, the past is out of reach and after this, there is nothing.’ I can hardly imagine a greater gift than to be here now, to be. And to be (where we exist) is in our attention. If we must do anything in life it is to pay attention. To listen, to look, to smell, taste, touch... We must pay attention to others — that is often just called love. Not just every human, but every living thing before us has had, for the short duration of their lives, to pay attention.

“Do stuff. Be clenched, curious. Not waiting for inspiration’s shove or society’s kiss on your forehead. Pay attention. It’s all about paying attention. Attention is vitality. It connects you with others. It makes you eager. Stay eager.” - Susan Sag

As I look at the birds jumping from branch to branch, listening and responding to each other’s call, they orchestrate the chaotic dance which underscores my own life. There are encounters and rejections, every move is transient. There are no guarantees and no predictions, not even the tree will lay still. There is this tension which comes from landing on a new branch that says to me “I haven’t made this place my home, I am just taking a short rest. I am looking around and percieving it all. When you look back at me, I will already be gone.” We are the space between the nothing before and after, in that sense, we are the everything we will ever come to know. And if there is to be any meaning in anything, it must be in that space, as there cannot be any in the nothingness. And the birds, what do they do after fleeting this branch? They jump to the next one, and they repeat it. They look around, they perceive it all. They may fly to a different tree, or stop for a bit of food. They may look for sticks or mate to reproduce. But at any given moment, they retain that transient tension. They are detached from where they are standing, yet attached to the moment and space they occupy. All they need is themselves, any surface, branch, phone line, or roof will do. With birds in constant movement, I feel stuck when I don’t move, yet that transient tension that I envy, lies in their self-assurance to fly away.
