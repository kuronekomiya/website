It's not about social media II:
Living without a
smartphone in Beijing.

Thursday, 9th of March, 2026

Note to the reader: This is a continuation to the article “Exploring too much choice in streaming platforms,” where I detail my experience moving away from Spotify and Substack a year ago. In this article, I link choice overload to the wider ways in which smartphones affect us beyond social media use.

Choice saturation in streaming platforms is just an example of the many ways in which contemporary technology affects us. We are only beginning to study some of the deeper cognitive effects smartphones themselves have on our psyche, all of which go beyond social media use. For example, keeping a smartphone in your pocket or in another room will affect test scores, even if it is never looked at. And a different study showed people experience lower interpersonal trust with strangers when a switched-off smartphone was placed on a table. Humans, who are deeply perceptive and take pride on the intelligence which has enabled them to create these technologies in the first place, somehow seem quite blind to this. We may be too clever for our own good, thinking we can fight this through willpower alone.

There are many distinctions between computing technology and other tools. While most tools act as extensions of our bodies (for example, a hammer is an extension of the hand) or senses (glasses are an extension of our vision), I argue that smartphones can be considered a much deeper extension of our minds. Interestingly, it’s Chinese name is shouji (lit. hand-machine), and hands are the first extension of our thinking onto our environment (hence why we gesticulate as we speak; it has been shown that hand movement can be a tool for thinking).

Within communication technologies, smartphones are unique in the degree of involvement and number of choices presented. Talking on the tele-phone (distance + sound) or watching tele-vision (distance + sight) can be seen as an extension of our speech and sight, but the complexity of the interaction and the decision making required to order food at a restaurant, call an uber, and Paypal a friend makes smartphones much more entangled into our psyche than we realise. This involvement can be seen in our body language; while we lean back when we watch a movie, we lean in when we use our phones, it captures much more of our attention. There may be something about holding all of human knowledge in your pocket as you share some coffee with a friend which may be subconsciously drawing you in. It’s a lot of weight to be carrying around.

When I have told people that I have quit using my smartphone in Beijing (arguably the most technologically-dependent city in the world), I have been berated. They have said that service workers hate me, that I am inconveniencing everyone and that I will be left in the dust. The most curious of the bunch may even ask “but how do you buy food?” I shrug and naively say that as long as you still have an internet connection at home and a mobile phone to receive SMS’s we don’t actually need a smartphone as much as we are told. Would they believe me if I said I actually enjoy the inconvenience of taking the bus? Yet, why choose this pitiable existence? Am I a recovering addict? Someone completely devoid of willpower? While some may find it impressive, others think it is pathetic. I have been told to simply “delete social media” instead (something I did many years ago). To get rid of the VPN, but continue using WeChat, AliPay, TaoBao, AMap and MeiTuan, the five basic apps you need to be “alive” in China. Yet, I lack the tools to disarm their argument, I do not know how to describe the numbness, impulsivity, irritability, lack of creativity which comes with it. All I can muster is “I am very sensitive to the effects of smartphones, that is why I cannot have one” rather than attribute this to a wider issue.
